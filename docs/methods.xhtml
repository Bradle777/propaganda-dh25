Methods

Markup Standards

In our project, we use a structured markup strategy to categorize presidential rhetoric across a politically dynamic timespan. The "policy" element is our primary element, and it encodes several key features through attributes: 'type' (foreign, domestic, or economic), 'connotation' (positive, negative, or neutral), 'when' (future, past, or present), and 'value' (the specific policy area, such as immigration, military, health, etc.). A "policy" may also include repeated instances of "anthro", which codes anthropological themes such as scarcity, inGroup, outGroup, authority, trajectory, transcendence, unity, division, time, role, or obligation, as well as optional "audience" reactions when relevant.

The "audience" element records how the in-person audience responded, using the 'noise' attribute to identify applause, booing, or other reactions. Whenever a speech includes cheering, booing, or an identifiable outburst, we encoded it through "audience".

We also use the "ref" element to mark moments when the president briefly addresses individuals, groups, or anecdotes within the speech. A "ref" may contain mixed content and nested "audience" elements when the crowd directly responds to the digression.

Together, these elements and attributes form a consistent markup system that captures both the thematic content of presidential messaging and the real-time reactions of the audience.

Example: <policy type="d" connotation="positive" when="present" value="pa" anthro="obligation">

Interpretation: This indicates the president is making a domestic policy statement, framed with a positive tone, referring to the present, specifically about public assistance (pa), and evoking the broader anthropological/human theme of obligation.

Qualitative Interpretations

Many of our markup choices regarding the identification of political attributes were straightforward, but the assignment of anthropological tags required more subjectivity. In our markup system, anthropological attributes let us tag the deeper rhetorical instincts that presidents rely on when transforming policy into narrative.
For example, when a speech uses 'scarcity', the speaker is framing a policy challenge as a resource crisis—linking it to political themes like inflation, budget deficits, or unemployment, all of which the schema maps directly to "scarcity" or downward economic "trajectory". Likewise, when a president invokes 'obligation', they’re distributing responsibility: the rules show obligation is tied to an 'agent'—citizens, Congress, families, leadership—so political appeals to “duty,” “responsibility,” or “service” are all encoded as versions of "obligation".

Below is a simplified association list showing how each anthropological attribute aligns with common political rhetorical moves:

• 'scarcity' → political fears around inflation, deficits, energy shortages, unemployment
• 'inGroup' → praise of “the American people,” workers, families, veterans, allies
• 'outGroup' → enemies, foreign adversaries, criminals, political opponents
• 'authority' → invoking Congress, law enforcement, scientific experts, Founders, or security institutions
• 'trajectory' → “America rising,” “national decline,” progress vs. decay claims
• 'transcendence' → moral virtue, religious framing, humanitarian sacrifice, ideological destiny
• 'unity' → bipartisan cooperation, national togetherness, international partnership
• 'division' → partisan conflict, institutional tension, moral polarization
• 'time' → appeals to tradition, crisis in the present, or future national promise
• 'role' → identifying specific societal groups (citizens, leadership, families, military) as actors
• 'obligation' → duties placed on Congress, citizens, government, parents, or leadership

This list-based method keeps our markup consistent. Each anthropological value corresponds to predictable political moves, allowing us to encode rhetorical strategy.

Quantitative Methods

Our quantitative workflow begins with automation, but does not attempt automated interpretation. The original speech transcripts were sourced from the White House, which already provides the text in a stable paragraph structure. Using Python, we automated the creation of a markup “scaffold” by wrapping each paragraph in a consistent container format and inserting empty, well-formed placeholder elements using the attribute categories defined above. This step mainly served to standardize the corpus and prevent formatting drift across presidencies and source pages. Importantly, while the XML format and paragraph structure were automated, the actual assignment of political attributes (type, value, connotation, when) and anthropological tags was still performed by hand by our group in order to preserve interpretive accuracy.

Once markup was complete, we generated visual summaries in the Analysis section using SVG (Scalable Vector Graphics). Each graph functions as a simple frequency plot, where the x-axis corresponds to a specific speech date and the y-axis corresponds to the number of times a given category appears in that speech. The “bars” in each chart are SVG rect elements whose height is proportional to the count we recorded, while axis labels, tick marks, and gridlines are SVG text and line elements. Because SVG is native to the browser, these graphics remain lightweight and transparent in their construction. In other words, the charts are not images. They are directly readable visual encodings of the same markup counts represented elsewhere in the site.

Finally, the site’s interactive tools were built using inline JavaScript and CSS. In the Texts section, the highlighting mechanism works by toggling classes or inline styles on the marked spans associated with policy elements, allowing categories to be visually isolated without reloading or rewriting the underlying document. The Statistics menu follows the same logic. Selecting a topic or attribute triggers a function that reveals the corresponding statistical block while hiding the others, making the quantitative summaries navigable and comparable within the same page view. These mechanisms are intentionally simple: the goal is not to hide complexity behind a black box, but to provide an interface where a user can quickly move between broad frequency patterns and specific textual evidence.
