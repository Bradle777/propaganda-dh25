Forming Conclusions

The core of this project is to create a tool with which one can generate novel conclusions.

While we have presented statistical overlap between attributes in the Analysis section, those patterns serve more as a standard to identify consistent patterns across the 43-year corpus.

Below, we describe three major lenses through which to interpret rhetoric patterns and form conclusions.

Political Relativist View

Follows the logic that political propaganda is mostly in response to the contemporary political climate.

Economic Messaging in the Early-to-Mid 2000s

Here, it seems obvious that economic optimism would rise after a recession. However, the Political Relativist view also reconciles the lack of economic interest before the recession. We see that political messaging can be interpreted as a guage of contemporary climate, and thus as a predictor of the future climate.



Social Issue Messaging in the Early-to-Mid 2000s

In less quantifiable areas like Social Issues, a purely Political view yields diminishing returns. We can see some increased social optimism in the years following the beginning of the 2nd Gulf war, but the correlation (and thus the model's ability for prediction) is not as strong or consistent.

Anthropological View

Follows the idea that propaganda is just adapted forms of basic human group rhetoric.
