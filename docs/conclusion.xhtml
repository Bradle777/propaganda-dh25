Forming Conclusions

The core of this project is to create a tool with which one can generate novel conclusions.

While we have presented statistical overlap between attributes in the Analysis section, those patterns
serve more as a standard to identify consistent patterns across the 43-year corpus.

Below, we describe three major lenses through which to interpret rhetoric patterns and form conclusions.

Political Relativist View

When adopting a political relativist view to form one’s own conclusions, the primary advantage is practicality. This lens aligns cleanly with the site’s statistical structure, particularly the attribute counts and co-occurrence menus in the Texts section. Because political framing tends to track observable conditions, such as economic instability or wartime transitions, its signals are relatively easy to quantify and compare. A user working across many speeches can quickly identify patterns in emphasis, optimism, or urgency without needing to interpret each instance in isolation. In this sense, political messaging functions as a readable surface layer, one that can be sampled broadly and still yield coherent trends.

This statistical accessibility becomes increasingly important as the number of speeches grows. The political view scales well because it relies on frequency, distribution, and correlation rather than fine-grained interpretation. When applied to large samples, shifts in political rhetoric become more reliable, not because individual speeches are perfectly representative, but because aggregate patterns smooth out rhetorical noise. For users attempting to draw conclusions from decades of addresses, this makes the political relativist approach not only faster, but often more defensible. The conclusions it supports are grounded in visible changes across time rather than in the internal logic of any single speech.

That said, the same features that make this view easy to apply also define its limits. Political context can explain when rhetoric changes, but not always how leaders choose to frame those changes. Even when relying on statistical summaries, users will still encounter moments where similar conditions produce different rhetorical strategies. At that point, the political view describes the shift without fully accounting for the persuasive mechanics behind it. Used on its own, it encourages conclusions that are stable and broadly supported, but sometimes shallow. Its strength lies in scale and comparability, not in capturing the deeper constraints that shape how leaders balance optimism, criticism, responsibility, and blame within the rhetoric itself.

Economic Messaging in the Early-to-Mid 2000s

Here, it seems obvious that economic optimism would rise after a recession. However, the Political Relativist view also reconciles the lack of economic interest before the recession. We see that political messaging can be interpreted as a gauge of contemporary climate, and thus as a predictor of the future climate.



Social Issue Messaging in the Early-to-Mid 2000s

In less quantifiable areas like Social Issues, a purely political view yields diminishing returns. We can see some increased social optimism as the Nation moves away from the chaotic start of the 2nd Gulf War, but the correlation (and thus the model's ability for prediction) is not as strong or consistent.

Anthropological View

When applying an anthropological view to form conclusions, the user is encouraged to look past surface-level topics and instead focus on the recurring problems leaders are trying to solve through rhetoric. From this perspective, the value category attached to a passage matters less than the structural function it serves within a group narrative. Regardless of whether a speech is about economics, immigration, or foreign affairs, leaders must continually legitimize authority by balancing fear with reassurance and criticism with control. This approach is particularly useful for identifying patterns that repeat across administrations, even when political conditions differ. The escalation pairing in the Trump 2018 example illustrates this clearly. The technique works not because of immigration as a policy domain, but because it compresses historical harm into present danger and then suspends resolution, keeping the leader positioned as a necessary stabilizing force.

One advantage of this view is that it allows users to recognize persuasive strategies that would otherwise appear inconsistent or contradictory. Similar rhetorical sequences can emerge in response to very different circumstances, suggesting that they are driven less by context than by shared expectations about leadership and group security. The Obama 2009 threat resolution ritual provides a clear example. Past economic failure establishes danger, present authority claims the ability to act, and future benefits finalize legitimacy. Read anthropologically, this sequence is effective because it fulfills a basic group demand for closure. The specifics of the economy matter less than the fact that uncertainty is acknowledged, addressed, and symbolically resolved.

At the same time, the anthropological view has clear limitations when used in isolation. By prioritizing structure over circumstance, it can flatten distinctions that are politically meaningful and obscure why certain topics dominate at particular moments. While mechanisms like escalation pairing and threat resolution rituals can be identified reliably, they do not on their own explain why a leader chooses one domain over another, or why certain narratives resonate more strongly in specific historical contexts. As a result, this lens is best suited for understanding how persuasion operates rather than for predicting when it will be deployed. Used alone, it reveals the architecture of rhetoric, but not the external pressures that determine which rooms are entered and which are left untouched.

Academic View

In media stat virtus. - Thomas Aquinas


The Academic View attempts to merge the Political and Antrhopological approaches, and achieve a repeatable method for interpreting rhetorical behavior across the corpus.


The process starts by isolating a single anthropological mechanism and holding it constant across cases, which prevents stylistic differences from being mistaken for fundamentally different rhetorical strategies. In the example on the right, by breaking each instance of the mechanism into attributes such as topic domain and temporal orientation, it is possible to see how the same mechanism can function either as soft norm consolidation or as hard boundary enforcement. Counting these instances across an entire presidency then allows numerical patterns to be read alongside qualitative shifts in attribute usage. This is where the Political Relativist and Anthropological views merge: differences in frequency, explicitness, and attribute progression can be interpreted as responses to political context, legitimacy pressure, or audience volatility without assuming intent or moral judgment.


The example conclusion that one president is rhetorically more conservative than another can therefore be framed as a hypothesis about how cautiously or aggressively moral boundaries are constructed under different political conditions.
